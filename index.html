The problem we address
The advisory gap in agri-food AI
"Data exists — but the right knowledge does not reach the right actor at the right moment in a form they can trust and act on."
Opacity
Most deployed AI tools cannot explain their reasoning — undermining farmer trust and creating regulatory liability under the EU AI Act.
Corpus bias
AI systems retrieve from existing knowledge bases. If those bases reflect structural inequities, the tool reproduces them at scale — invisibly.
Governance gaps
GDPR, EU AI Act, and agricultural data space standards are not yet coherently integrated into agri-food AI development pipelines.
Adoption failure
Technically sound tools fail at scale because end-users — smallholder farmers, women in agri-food — are not in the design loop.

From prototype to programme

KB WP4 provides what most NWO proposals cannot: empirical evidence that the approach works, a functioning system to build on, and a team with direct field experience in the contexts where AI advisory tools are most needed.

The proposed NWO project extends this in three directions: scaling the architecture to Dutch agri-food contexts; deepening the governance layer to meet EU AI Act and data space requirements; and expanding comparatively to LMIC contexts where gender and advisory dynamics differ fundamentally.

KB WP4 is not a co-funder. It is the research base — and it is already running.

The dimension most proposals miss
Gender equity & structural justice in AI
The corpus problem
RAG systems retrieve from existing agronomic literature — shaped by decades of gender-blind, technocratic research. Bias is architectural, not incidental.
Co-design as method
Participatory workshops grounded in Ethics of Care and Deep Democracy serve as structured inputs to debias the knowledge architecture — with IFPRI and FAO providing LMIC field access.
Pilot-to-scale logic
Dutch proof-of-concept → comparative LMIC extension where gender roles, land rights, and advisory dynamics look fundamentally different.

Existing literature on AI in agri-food carries a persistent technocratic, saviour-complex orientation — focused narrowly on hardware access, largely centred on African women, and silent on the structural conditions that determine whether AI tools actually benefit them.

This consortium integrates an ELSA lens from the design stage. The question is not only "does this tool work?" but "who does it work for, who does it leave out — and why?" Those are research questions, not compliance checkboxes.

With IFPRI and FAO as international partners, the consortium has direct access to LMIC contexts across Sub-Saharan Africa and South-East Asia — where the same advisory system will encounter radically different gender dynamics, land tenure structures, and institutional landscapes.

Most proposals in this call will address the Dutch domestic context. This consortium does that — and tests further.
